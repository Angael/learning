## Expected reasoning

- One or two coherent supported Perfekt forms satisfy the tense requirement; examples include `Ich habe gestern gearbeitet.`, `Ich habe eingekauft.`, or `Ich habe einen Film gesehen.`
- In a subordinate clause, the finite verb comes at the end: `Ich bin zu Hause, weil ich müde bin.` or `Ich glaube, dass ich heute zu Hause bleibe.`
- A fronted `weil` clause is acceptable with main-clause inversion: `Weil ich müde bin, bleibe ich heute zu Hause.`
- In the transformation, `die Datei` is feminine Akkusativ and becomes `sie`.
- Only that phrase changes. The Dativ noun phrase remains `der Ärztin`.
- With an Akkusativ pronoun and a remaining Dativ noun phrase, the expected sentence is `Ich schicke sie der Ärztin.`

## Acceptable variants

- Any three understandable recent-life sentences that meet the declared constraints; they need not use every supplied item.
- Either `weil` or a natural `dass` frame is acceptable.
- `zu Hause` and `zuhause` are both acceptable.
- `ae/oe/ue/ss` may replace German characters.
- Minor capitalization, punctuation, or low-value lexical issues do not outweigh correct target morphology and word order.
- Supplied Perfekt forms make this supported production; do not infer independent tense recall.

## Likely misconception

- Main-clause order inside the subordinate clause: `weil ich bin müde` instead of `weil ich müde bin`.
- After a fronted subordinate clause: `Weil ich müde bin, ich bleibe ...` instead of `..., bleibe ich ...`.
- Replacing both complements or writing a two-pronoun sentence.
- Choosing Dativ `ihr` for `die Datei` instead of Akkusativ `sie`.
- Keeping noun-before-pronoun order: `Ich schicke der Ärztin sie.`
- Using more than two Perfekt forms and increasing avoidable tense load.
